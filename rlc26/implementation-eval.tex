\section{Experimental Results}
We conduct experiments to show the superior performance of our method compared to the baselines. We also perform ablations with respect to all the key components of the framework; and draw insights on their importance and how we can tune them.

\textbf{Learning dynamics.}\quad \Cref{fig:learning-curves} shows the learning curves for multi-policy bidding and single-policy PPO algorithms on the two environments. 
While the curves are more noisy with Atari Assault, they exhibit clearly distinguishable sigmoidal curves with Dynamic Targets. 
The curves for All-Pay and Winner-Pays methods start growing around the same time.
Meanwhile, the All-Pay method with only local observations for each policy starts growing much earlier but plateaus below All-Pay.
The All-Pay variant with no attention pooling for targets only starts growing much later.
This validates the use of the attention pooling architecture to learn useful embeddings of other targets.
We also remark that, based on the multi-agent PPO recipe of \citet{yu2022mappo}, we also found that using a small clip coefficient (\({\sim}0.05\)) and large batch sizes was crucial to obtain slower but stable convergence of the policies towards a joint-equilibrium.

\textbf{Comparison with baselines}\quad \Cref{tab:all_methods_comp} lists the performance of our method as well as the baselines on both environments. 
Our bidding-based multi-policy approaches outperform the baselines by a big margin.
Across both the environments, we speculate that the poor performance of the single-policy PPO learner is due to the difficulty of credit assignment over a rollout in which there are multiple objectives that provide a positive reward.
In the Dynamic Targets environment, we observe that the All-Pay multi-policy method with no reward shaping performs poorly.
This indicates the difficulty of learning to navigate in the large state space with only a sparse reward signal.
In the Atari Assault environment, there is a delay between shooting a missile until it reaches the alien ship and destroys it. 
Since we only supply information from one frame in the observation, the learner could find it hard to associate a firing of a missile and the reward from the death of a ship, especially if there is subsequent firing.
On the other hand, the credit assignment becomes easier when we separate the objectives and each learner only has to focus on one target.

Next, let us try to reason about the poor performance of the DWN baseline.
We recall that this method learns a W-network trained to predict the negative TD-error for each policy when the policy does not play its action.
However, in our environments, we have a long horizon and all the objectives are symmetric with similar Q-values.
This means that the TD-error is small and does not provide a strong signal to choose between policies at most timesteps.
In contrast, our approach allows the policies themselves to indicate the importance of playing an action.

\textbf{Interpretability of policies and bidding behaviors.}\quad
Video rollouts of the bidding-based policies in both environments, included in the supplementary material, demonstrate that the policies bid to take control of the agent when they can accumulate rewards in the action window.
We can also observe that each policy only seeks to fulfill its own objectives, which is what we desire.
\Cref{fig:control-bid-dist} shows the distribution of the number of timesteps that each policy was in control and the distribution over bids placed by the policies.
We observe that there is a good spread of control amongst all the agents.
With respect to the bid distribution, we observe different behaviors in the two environments.
In the Atari Assault environment, all the policies bid 0 most of the time indicating that the agents are able to maximize their rewards through random assignment of control amongst the agents and do not need to place bids.
This also intuitively makes sense as the alien ships do not automatically expire unlike the targets in Dynamic Targets.
In contrast, with the Dynamic Targets environment, there is a wider spread of bids between 0 and 6. 
Here, we see that the All-Pay mechanism produces more conservative policies that tend to bid 0 most of the time with decreasing number of bids between 1-6.
Meanwhile, the Winner-Pays mechanism has an even spread across all the possible bids indicating that the policies are willing to take risks and bid higher more often.

\textbf{Scaling number of targets.}\quad We conduct an experiment with the Dynamic Targets environment in which we scale the number of targets at deployment time, i.e., we create more copies of the policy to compete with each other. 
\Cref{fig:bidding-mechanism-scaling} shows that the policies continue to perform well up to 14 targets even though they were only trained to compete with up to 7 other policies.
The attention pooling mechanism plays a crucial role here as it can flexibly generate useful embeddings of an arbitrary number of competing targets.

\textbf{Further ablations.}\quad \Cref{fig:gridworld-bid-upper-bound} plots the performance of policies trained with different ranges of bids. 
We see that we need at least 2 levels of bids to achieve good performance.
Up to just 1 bid level, the policies are unable to effectively compete with each other.
Similarly, \Cref{fig:gridworld-bid-penalty} tries different values for the bid penalty factor.
Without any penalty, the performance is low since all the policies always bid the highest trying to take control.
However, if it is too big, then the policies are not willing to take the risk of placing the bid. 
Finally, \Cref{fig:action-window} compares policies trained with varying sizes of action windows.
In both environments, we observe an ideal size maximizing the performance.
When it is lower than this value, the control is switching between policies too fast before they are able to reach their targets.
On the other hand, if it is too high, then there is not enough switching between policies to maximize all the objectives.
