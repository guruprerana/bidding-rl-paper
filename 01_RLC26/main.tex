\summary{
We consider multi-objective reinforcement learning problems where objectives come from an identical family---such as the class of reachability objectives---and may appear or disappear at runtime.
At any moment, an arbitrary number of objectives may be active, and we make no assumptions about their distribution or arrival pattern.
Our goal is to design adaptive policies that can efficiently adjust their behaviors as the set of active objectives changes.
This problem has many practical use cases, like online replanning of paths for warehouse robots under dynamically arriving tasks. While similar problems have been extensively studied in the planning literature---typically assuming that a precise model of the environment (e.g., a graph) is available---in reinforcement learning settings, where the environment may be uncertain and its model unavailable, this problem has received little attention to the best of our knowledge.


To solve this problem, we propose a novel modular framework where each objective is supported by a selfish local policy, and coordination is achieved through a novel \emph{auction}-based mechanism:
policies bid for the right to execute their actions, with bids reflecting the urgency of the current state.
The highest bidder selects the action, enabling a dynamic and interpretable trade-off among objectives.
Going back to the adaptation problem, when objectives change, the system adapts by simply adding or removing the corresponding policies.
Moreover, as objectives arise from the same family, identical copies of a generalized policy can be reused, facilitating immediate adaptation.
In contrast, monolithic policies serving all objectives lacks this flexibility.
Additionally, when the objective count varies, we empirically demonstrate that both in terms of training stability and performance, our modular approach significantly outperforms the monolithic baseline.
}
