\section{Introduction}

A large class of real-world control problems requires the simultaneous fulfillment of multiple, partially conflicting objectives from the identical family.
We study a setting in which objectives come from the discounted cumulative rewards family, and during deployment, they can appear or disappear without any prior information about their arrival process.

As a running example, consider a mobile feeding robot deployed in a large cat shelter. The robot is responsible for delivering food to cats roaming freely around the facility. Each feeding request is modeled as a discounted payoff objective, reflecting the fact that serving a hungry cat sooner yields higher reward than serving it later. New requests may arise at arbitrary locations and times as cats become hungry and start meowing, while existing requests may disappear if a cat wanders away before being served.
At any moment, an arbitrary number of feeding requests may be active, and we make no assumptions about their spatial distribution or arrival pattern. Since the robot can serve only one cat at a time, fulfilling one request may delay others, potentially allowing some cats to move farther away. The robot’s policy must therefore adapt dynamically to achieve an optimal trade-off among the payoffs associated with the currently active requests. Our goal is to design lightweight, adaptive policies that update their behavior seamlessly as objectives appear or disappear.


This problem is of significant practical importance and has been extensively studied in the planning literature~\citep{fox2006plan}. 
However, most existing approaches assume a precise environment model and the absence of uncertainty. 
In contrast, within reinforcement learning (RL) settings---where the environment may be uncertain and a model may not be available---this problem has not been studied to the best of our knowledge.

We propose a novel policy adaptation algorithm tailored to this class of problems. 
The central idea is a compositional design for multi-objective RL, in which each objective is supported by an independent policy, and coordination is achieved through an online auction-based mechanism. 
At every decision step, policies submit bids for the right to execute their actions, where bids reflect the urgency of the current state with respect to their objective. 
The highest bidder selects the action, enabling a dynamic and interpretable trade-off among objectives.

Returning to the cat feeder example, suppose the robot approaches an intersection with two active requests. 
The policy for the first request recommends turning left, while the policy for the second recommends turning right. 
Since both actions cannot be executed simultaneously, a trade-off is unavoidable. The auction mechanism determines which objective is prioritized, based on the relative urgency encoded in the bids.

This compositional design yields strong modularity. When an objective appears or disappears, only the corresponding policy needs to be added or removed, without modifying the remainder of the system. 
Furthermore, when objectives belong to the identical family---which in our case is discounted cumulative rewards---a single generalized policy can be trained for the entire family. 
New objectives can then be instantiated as identical copies of this policy, enabling immediate adaptation. 
In contrast, a monolithic policy serving all objectives lacks this flexibility. 
Training such a policy under a variable number of objectives leads to substantial degradation in both training stability and performance, as we demonstrate empirically.

We show how classical RL policies can be augmented with bidding capabilities. 
A natural approach is to extend the action space with numeric bid values; however, this introduces three key challenges.

\textbf{Challenge I: Enforcing honest bids.}
Policies must bid in proportion to their true urgency. 
Otherwise, they may learn to overbid systematically in order to suppress competitors, resulting in dysfunctional behavior. 
To prevent this, we impose penalties during training that are proportional to the bid values. 
As a consequence, excessive bidding reduces the net return of a policy, aligning its incentives with truthful urgency estimation.

\textbf{Challenge II: Achieving environment awareness.}
Effective bidding depends not only on the current state and the local objective, but also on the configuration of competing objectives. 
For example, in the cat feeder problem, if two requests are spatially close, their policies need not compete aggressively. Conversely, if requests lie in opposite directions, the more urgent policy must bid sufficiently high to prevail. 
To enable such environment-aware bidding, we formulate local policy learning as a multi-agent nonzero-sum game, where each agent represents an objective and learns to optimize its payoff in the presence of competing agents.

\textbf{Challenge III: Unbounded objective count.}
During deployment, the number of active objectives may exceed those observed during training. 
This invalidates standard game-theoretic training formulations, which assume a fixed number of opponents. 
To address this issue, we employ an attention mechanism that aggregates an arbitrary number of competing objectives from the given family into a fixed-dimensional representation. 
This encoding is then provided as input to the policy network. 
Each local policy is equipped with its own attention module, co-trained with the policy across progressively increasing numbers of objectives. 


We implement our framework using proximal policy optimization (PPO) as the base learning algorithm. On the cat feeder benchmark, modeled using a gridworld-based path-planning problem, and on the Atari Assault benchmark, our approach demonstrates significantly improved training stability and superior performance compared to monolithic baselines. 
In particular, compositional training converges faster and exhibits substantially lower variance in returns, highlighting the practical benefits of modular auction-based coordination.
